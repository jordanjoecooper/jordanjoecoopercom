Projects Are Gases, Not Bricks

01/06/2026

[DRAFT, still public like all are but hidden from menu :) Hi if you find this with AI or crawling the output]

Most people think about projects as if they are bricks. Get the foundations right and  get stacking.

A brick has known dimensions, known weight and known properties. If you need one hundred bricks, you can count them. If you need two hundred, you can count those too.

Projects rarely behave this way.

Instead, projects often behave more like gases.

The Properties of a Gas

  • Gases expand to fill the available space.
  • Pressure increases when volume decreases.
  • Gases are difficult to observe at the individual particle level.
  • Small changes in conditions can have surprisingly large effects.

Scope Expands to Fill the Container

A project given two months often takes two months.
 The same project given six months often somehow takes six months.
Explore:
  • New requirements
  • Edge cases
  • Stakeholder feedback
  • Additional quality expectations
  • Gold plating
Note should consider connecting this to Parkinson’s Law.

Deadlines Are Pressure

When the container gets smaller:
  • Stress increases
  • Communication quality decreases
  • Decision quality changes
  • Risk taking increases
Note: look at the difference between productive pressure and destructive pressure, it is a fine balance.

Estimation Measures Uncertainty, Not Effort

Building something you’ve built ten times before is very different to building something nobody in your team or company understands. The effort required may be similar the uncertainty is not.
Note add section here on why experienced engineers often reduce estimates by reducing unknowns rather than working faster.

Complexity Is Entropy

Left alone, projects naturally become more complicated , requirements drift, dependencies increase, more and more people become involved and exceptions accumulate.

Note here to talk about why complexity is the default state and simplicity requires active effort.

Communication as a Pressure Relief Valve - Note to link to Persian messenger syndrome

Many project failures are not technical failures.
They fail because of:
  • Hidden assumptions
  • Poor communication
  • Delayed escalation
  • Missing context
Which then means that information flow often matters more than raw engineering output. - Note explore further, more reading.

The Jevons Paradox of Software - Link to other note.
If software becomes cheaper to create through AI:
  • Do we build less software?
  • Or do we simply build more?
Note : Explore how the bottleneck moves from code production to judgement, prioritization and understanding.

Conclusion: The Shape of Uncertainty

Projects are not piles of bricks waiting to be counted. They are dynamic systems filled with uncertainty, constraints and changing conditions. Great teams do not estimate perfectly but they do reduce uncertainty quickly.
Think of it like this the scope is the gas. Deadlines are the big old container and the uncertainty is the pressure.
