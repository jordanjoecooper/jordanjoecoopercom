Work and artificial intelligence

23/02/2026
Last Updated: 1st June 2026.

Not too long ago (in terms of human history ) the way to discover new information was to read a book or a series of papers. This was quickly followed by wires and signals which soon gave way to a connected web and new hardware (keyboards and screens) plus new methods of indexing. Search being the biggest among them across the web, you wait for a website to show you a list of potential answers to your question, this has been the normal for quite some time in. Finally even that approach is now displaced. Now a website or app shows you a summary for your query tailored by artificial intelligence. In fact, many people are using LLM-based chat interfaces in place of search engines in the browser.

The future

We outsourced the task of sifting through shelves of books and papers to algorithms and search engines. Now humans are beginning to do the same for the selection of that information, something they previously did by hand and eye themselves. We are outsourcing a large chunk of the sorting mechanism.

But that is not all. In tech circles we tend to be overzealous in our predictions of the future. However, the future often ends up not too far away from the wild predictions, in this instance programmers are one of the first waves to be deeply affected both positively and negatively, they are the first in a generational change that will continue across any knowledge-based or repeatable predominantly computer-based tasks.

Of course there are unresolved issues on either side of this change, for example:

  • What happens when scaling requirements of pre-training and inference of large languages models overtake hardware production or power capacity? - Worse still, our ability to create new power capacity? In line, will the scaling law of the models continue to exponentially hold up?
  • How will different nations with varying levels of economic situation and reliance on technology adopt increasingly black-box seemingly magical systems?
  • Assuming improvement, automation, scale and adoption accelerate to where many believe they can, what happens to the existing western capitalist model when both labour demand falls and services based economies begin to consolidate? Or does Jeavons Paradox run a mock here?
  • Should one model company succeed and achieve some form of advanced monopoly (unlikely at present but possiblem likely 2-3 per hemisphere). What then? Both for corporations and more globally nations and their people? Hypothetically, artificial general intelligence (AGI) could be so powerful it becomes a larger global threat as a weapon system than any conventional or nuclear weapon, what happens when this tool is not shared equally?
  • Will advancement outrun capital? Contrasting the advancement prediction and its capital cost, there is a question as to whether income generation plus research and development costs outpace available capital, will the technology race outrun the capital race? Or will the capital race outrun the technology race?

Speaking to different people outside of the tech bubble yields many different views. I've spoken to plumbers, bakers, builders, surveyors, lawyers, retirees and accountants. Many don't trust the existing tools that are much less sophisticated. Some just want tech to work, but work in a way that they can understand. That does not mean they want bespoke everything built just for them, reliability and predictability seem to be the order of the day , along with removing the monotonous and tideous. Many of these things could be achieved pre-ai with simple effective system design, yet taken as a global singularity, tech has often failed to deliver, that won't change here, good basics may be easier to implement with AI, we shall see.

Work will change. For better and for worse. Great vocal people will assume they can now do everything , without understanding much of the thing at all. Juniors can learn faster than ever, if they take the correct mental model and attitudes and people across all levels of experience must be way of hitting approve and not sacrifice the nine yards required to learn something through mental repetition, in some ways certain areas of work are being gamified with prompt > approve > done. This provides both extremely useful acceleration if you have good taste and existing knowledge and equally very poor almost expert looking outcomes that some will never be able to explain even at a surface level, its unclear how we filter both of those outcomes and all of the others that hang off of them.
