Moving Fast vs Looking Fast

01/06/2026

We celebrate speed in modern organizations. "Move fast," "bias for action," and "high agency" have become markers of strong performance. But somewhere along the way, many people started optimizing for the appearance of speed rather than actual progress. They jump into conversations before understanding the problem, create activity before establishing direction, and make visible decisions that generate more work than they eliminate. Ironically, the more they try to signal momentum, the more friction they introduce. What looks like acceleration from the outside often becomes the very thing slowing the system down.

A common example is when a group spends time aligning on a problem. Multiple calls happen, options are discussed, trade-offs are understood, and everyone leaves with a clear plan of action. Then one person decides to go off-script. Instead of understanding why the agreed approach was chosen, they make a series of on-the-fly decisions alone, break something, fail to communicate what they’ve changed, and eventually report back only the one detail that supports their position while omitting the wider context. Suddenly, what should have been a straightforward one-hour task turns into multiple unplanned calls, competing plans, and a room full of confused people trying to reconstruct what the heck happened. 

The tragedy is that none of this was caused by a lack of effort or urgency. It was caused by someone being more concerned with appearing decisive and fast than understanding the problem well enough to solve it properly. Real velocity comes from reducing uncertainty and creating alignment. The moment you start optimizing for looking fast instead of being effective, you become a source of drag.

None of this means people should blindly follow a plan just because it was agreed in a meeting. In fact, some of the biggest improvements come from someone questioning the assumptions, challenging the requirements, or identifying a simpler path forward. 

The difference is in how it's done. The right approach is to come back with evidence, explain the reasoning and bring others along in the decision. "I think we've missed something here" is very different from "this is the way, just do it" One creates clarity; the other creates confusion. High-agency people don't ignore alignment—they improve it. They challenge decisions when necessary, but they do so transparently, with context, and with a genuine desire to get to a better outcome rather than simply proving they can move faster than everyone else. The goal isn't obedience to a plan. The goal is shared understanding of why the plan exists in the first place.
